Workers On Campus

A brief history

The university cannot function without us. Students and workers are the reason UW functions. Many students and student-workers have gone to exceptional lengths to stand up for workers’ rights. We provide the list of resistance in the past years in many forms, such as public protest, striking, and even posting memes online.

March 2000

Eighty percent of TAs signed union cards that gave the Graduate Student Employee Action Coalition (GSEAC) authority to represent them in labor negotiations with the University.

This made it so that The Graduate Student Employee Action Coalition/United Auto Workers (GSEAC/UAW) would officially represent UW teaching assistants, readers, graders, and tutors in collective bargaining.

More

October 25, 2000

UW still hadn’t recognized the union; John Sweeney, President of the American Federation of Labor and Congress of Industrial Organizations (AFL-CIO), spoke at UW to urge UW President McCormick to recognize the vote, supported by the Washington State Labor Council, the King County Labor Council, and state representatives; 86 percent of graduate TAs voted to strike to urge the administration to come to the table.

1
Early 2001

UW and TAs began to convince the Washington State Legislature to pass a collective bargaining bill that would allow GSEAC/UAW to bargain for wages and benefits based on productivity.

More

March 2002

Legislators passed the bill; it established a legal framework for student-employees to elect their union representation and bargain with UW.

April 1, 2010

Undergraduates and graduate TAs and research assistants gathered in Mary Gates Hall to protest proposed job cuts.

More

June 1, 2010

UAW and UW agreed to a one-year contract to meet the demand of TAs, maintaining the funding for Odegaard Writing and Research Center, paying for academic student-employees’ health insurance premiums, providing full tuition waivers to offset the costs of rising tuition and stagnant wages.

2011


The union ratified another one-year contract with the University; The contract maintained health insurance premiums for ASE, started a childcare reimbursement program for the, offset mandatory $50 of student fees by 2012.

June 2012

UW and UAW reached a three-year contract; the contract waived hundreds of dollars worth of fees that a neutral third party concluded violated the union’s collective bargaining rights, increased payment for the students who have to take out loans besides salaries to cover their need to live, increased child-care expense coverage, continued health care coverage, reimbursed salaried ASEs $50 for the student fees they paid from 2011-12.

May 1, 2014


Mayor Ed Murray announced that he brokered a deal to raise the minimum wage from $9.25 to $15 an hour, but the ordinance would not apply to the government institution such as UW.

May 23, 2014

Seattle unions gathered at UW Medical Center to rally for a $15 minimum wage.

More

April 12, 2015


The administration announced it would increase minimum wage for all student workers to $11 an hour; a large portion of the funding for student employment at UW comes from the Services and Activities Fee Committee (SAF), it would have to find an alternative way to fund the raise budget without raising the fees it collects from students. Seattle City Councilmember Kshama Sawant (seen in previous picture) suggested cutting the executives’ salaries.

April 2015

The three-year contract ended; The union held a meeting to hear from members about their concerns, which focused primarily on wages and mandatory fees. The union asked for training to combat microaggressions, raised wages, fee and tuition relief, improved health care coverage, and steps toward a more inclusive environment for academic student employees.

More
November 2, 2017

Workers at UW’s Consolidated Laundry facility in Mount Baker were put on notice that their jobs would be outsourced in March of 2018; UW entered into negotiations with the Hospital central Services Association to save UW Med an estimated $3 million a year in operation costs.

More
May 15, 2018

UAW carried through with its threat to strike. Many students called President Cauce’s offices to urge the administration to comply with the union's demands. The editorial board of the Daily and the Seattle City Council publicly supported UAW’s strike.

More
October 11, 2018

The Board of Regents approved in its Campus Master Plan a commitment to build an additional 450 units of affordable housing over the next 10 years.

More
2018-2019 Academic year

A delegation of union members and leaders delivered a letter to UW President, fighting for a fair and equitable contract that recognize the skyrocketing costs of living in the city, as well as the rampant inequity, harassment, the face that the university don’t have trans-inclusive healthcare for their trans members. Members of state and on-campus unions attended the rally outside of Gerberding Hall in Red Square to show solidarity and support with the negotiating workers.

More

2019


After the strike, UAW and UW reached a three-year agreement. The administration agreed to increase compensation by two percent for all ASEs, provide new sexual harassment training. UAW is hoping for better wage, lower fees, reductions in workplace harassment, and greater institutional inclusivity.

Conclusion:

Don’t believe what the University says! Institutions are slow. The UW is not structured to meet our needs, and definitely won’t do so without a push. This fight extends beyond the scope of the University, but that doesn’t mean that what we fight for at UW doesn’t matter!
